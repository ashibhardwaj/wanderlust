## Overview

Route: Tokyo, Hiroshima, Kyoto, Nara, Osaka.

Fly into Tokyo (Narita or Haneda), work west down the main island, and fly out of Osaka (Kansai, KIX). This is an open-jaw (multi-city) ticket, in to one city and out of another. A round-trip into a single city is fine too and is sometimes cheaper, but price the backtrack in before you book: if you end in Osaka but fly home from Tokyo, you pay for the Shinkansen back to Tokyo on your last day (around 14,000 to 15,000 yen and roughly 3 hours, or a short domestic flight). Open-jaw only loses if the round-trip fare is more than that gap cheaper, so do the sum on your own dates.

**Eight days is the bare minimum for Japan, and honestly still tight.** This is close to the trip I did, with Osaka given a full day instead of the rushed final morning I had, but I was still moving fast and skipping things I wanted to see. If you can, give it 10 to 14 days: a slower Kyoto, day trips as their own days rather than crammed in, and ideally a few days up in Hokkaido (more on that in [make it longer](#make-it-longer) at the end).

I went in February. Osaka was right at the very onset of cherry blossom, a few early branches, but February is really plum-blossom season, not sakura. For the blossom the country is famous for, aim for **late March to early April**, when the bloom sweeps north and Kyoto and Tokyo are at their peak. February has its own upside (smaller crowds, crisp clear days with the best odds of seeing Fuji), so it is a fair trade if you are not chasing the pink.

### Getting around

The Shinkansen (bullet train) links every stop on this route and is the backbone of the trip. The nationwide **JR Pass jumped in price in late 2023 and rarely pays off** for a route like this anymore, and it does not cover the fastest Nozomi trains anyway. I skipped it and bought Shinkansen tickets per leg; price your own itinerary before deciding, but do not assume the pass saves money the way older guides claim. For city trains, subways, and buses, just load a **Suica or ICOCA** IC card (or the version in Apple or Google Wallet) and tap through everything.

People are extraordinarily helpful. Station staff and the desks in big public buildings often keep handheld **translation devices**, so the language barrier is rarely a real wall; point, ask, and someone will sort you out.

### eSIM and money

I used a **Klook eSIM** and it connected the moment I landed, no airport queue and no SIM swap. Set it up before you fly and you walk off the plane already online.

Japan runs on **cash** far more than you would expect. Small restaurants, temples, shrines, markets, and some ticket machines are cash-only. I carried yen exchanged back in India, and credit cards.

### Shopping

Shop strategically and the tax works in your favour. Spend over **5,000 yen (pre-tax) at a single store on the same day** and the roughly 10% consumption tax is deducted at checkout, with no separate refund paperwork to chase at the airport later. So buy everything you want from one shop in one trip to clear the threshold. **Carry your passport** when you shop; they need it to ring up the tax-free sale.

What is worth the suitcase space:
- **Daiso** and **GU**: walk into either whenever you pass one. Great stuff at great prices, electronics and homeware in Daiso, clothes in GU.
- **Onitsuka Tiger**: the Japanese sneaker brand is noticeably cheaper here than back home, with styles you won't always find abroad.
- **KitKats**: an absurd variety of flavours you will not find anywhere else.
- **Matcha**: if anyone in the group is into it, it is everywhere and excellent.
- **Stationery and ceramics**: genuinely lovely, and easy to overdo.
- **Skincare**: if you are into it, Japanese and Korean skincare is everywhere, well priced, and the drugstores (Matsumoto Kiyoshi, Don Quijote) are stocked with it.
- **Thrift stores**: loads of them, often stocked with luxury-brand pieces in great condition.

Leave **luggage allowance for the way back**. The shopping adds up fast.

There is also a heavy **fan culture** here, so expect themed cafes, restaurants, and stores built around anime, characters, and franchises; fun to wander into even if you are not a fan.

### Vegetarian food

Japan is harder than most of Asia for vegetarians; people don't really understand vegetarianism as a concept. That said, you will not starve:

- **Convenience stores** (7-Eleven, Lawson, FamilyMart) are everywhere and have onigiri, sandwiches, and snacks; just check the fillings.
- **Indian restaurants** are common and a reliable fallback for a proper meal.

**Carry ready-to-eat meals** from home as much as you can. They save you on the long travel days and the late nights when nothing veg is open.

A nice extra: several hotels run a **happy hour with free drinks** (mocktails included). My Tokyo hotel (Hotel IL FIORE Kasai) had one. Check whether yours does when you book.

## Day 1, Tokyo

Land, drop your bags, and start on the east side of the city.

- **Sensoji** in Asakusa, Tokyo's oldest temple, sits at the back of a long market street (Nakamise). Give it proper time and explore the whole market area, not just the temple.
- **Tokyo Skytree** nearby for the view, if it is clear.
- Cross the city to **Shibuya** for the famous scramble crossing as it gets dark.
- Finish in **Shinjuku**. The **Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building** has a free observation deck with a 360-degree city view, and on clear days you can see Fuji from it. There is a large park within walking distance too.

## Day 2, Tokyo

A west-side and bay day.

- **Meiji Shrine** in its forest, then **Harajuku** and **Omotesando** for the people-watching and the themed cafes.
- **teamLab** (Planets or Borderless), the immersive digital-art museum. *Book tickets online in advance; it sells out and walk-up is rarely possible.*
- **Odaiba** on the bay for the waterfront park and the views back at the city.
- **Tokyo Tower** lit up after dark.

*If theme parks are your thing, **DisneySea** is unique to Tokyo and a whole day on its own; swap it in here and trim the rest.*

## Day 3, Fuji and Kawaguchiko day trip

Day trip out to the **Fuji Five Lakes**. **Lake Kawaguchiko** gives the postcard view of Mt. Fuji reflected in the water, best on a clear winter morning. Back in Tokyo for the evening.

*Alt: a **Hakone** day trip instead, the hot-spring town with the pirate ship across Lake Ashi, the ropeway over the volcanic valley, and Fuji views of its own. Pick one of the two if you only have the day; with more time, do both (see [make it longer](#make-it-longer)).*

## Day 4, Tokyo to Hiroshima

Morning Shinkansen west to **Hiroshima** (around 4 hours). Spend the afternoon at the **Peace Memorial Park**, the **Peace Memorial Museum**, and the **A-Bomb Dome**.

*A note on Hiroshima: some people find the museum heavy, even depressing, and skip the city for being out of the way. I understand that, but after the rush of Tokyo I found Hiroshima genuinely peaceful, and the history is worth sitting with. Go in knowing the museum is sombre, and give yourself a quiet evening after.*

## Day 5, Miyajima, then to Kyoto

Morning trip to **Miyajima** (a short train and ferry from Hiroshima) for **Itsukushima Shrine** and its torii gate standing in the water at high tide, with wild deer roaming the island. Back to Hiroshima, then the afternoon Shinkansen to **Kyoto** (around 2 hours). Evening in Kyoto: wander **Gion** and **Pontocho**, or the **Nishiki** market lanes.

## Day 6, Kyoto, then Osaka

Kyoto is wall-to-wall temples and shrines, more than a day can hold. The headline stops:

- **Fushimi Inari** at first light, the thousands of vermilion torii gates up the hillside. Go early to beat the crowds.
- **Arashiyama** bamboo grove, and the area around it.
- **Kinkakuji**, the golden pavilion.
- **Kyoto Imperial Palace** and its grounds.

*This is the day I most wished I had longer; there are many more temples and shrines, and Kyoto rewards a slower pace.*

In the evening, take the short train to **Osaka** (about half an hour) and check in; you will base here for the rest of the trip. Head out for your first night in **Dotonbori**, the canal-side food and neon district, and the **Shinsaibashi** shopping street next to it.

## Day 7, Osaka, and a Nara half-day

Spend the morning sightseeing in **Osaka**: **Osaka Castle** and its grounds are a good start. Nara is close enough to keep for the afternoon as a half-day trip rather than a stay.

In the afternoon, head out to **Nara** (about 45 minutes by train): the **deer park**, where the bowing deer roam free, and **Todaiji** temple with its giant bronze Buddha. Todaiji runs an occasional evening **light-and-sound show**; I caught one and it was worth staying for, so check whether one is on while you are there. Then the short train back to Osaka for the night.

*If there is no evening show on, do Nara earlier and keep the evening free for more of Osaka.*

## Day 8, Osaka, fly out

A last few hours in Osaka before the evening flight:
- **Umeda Sky Building** for the floating-garden observatory
- More of **Dotonbori** and **Shinsaibashi** for last-minute shopping and street food

Then to **Kansai (KIX)** for the flight home.

*If theme parks are your thing, **Universal Studios Japan** is in Osaka and a full day on its own. Slot it in here and do the city sights the evening before, or add another day.*

## Make it longer

Eight days is a starter route. If you can stretch it, here is where the extra days go furthest:

- **Slower Kyoto**: two or three days instead of one. Add Kiyomizu-dera, Ginkakuji and the Philosopher's Path, Tofukuji, and a day around the eastern Higashiyama district.
- **Day trips as their own days**: do both **Hakone** and **Fuji/Kawaguchiko** rather than choosing, and add **Nikko** (shrines and waterfalls north of Tokyo) and **Nara** as a full unhurried day.
- **A theme-park day**: **DisneySea** is unique to Tokyo (no other Disney resort in the world has it) and a full day on its own, and **Universal Studios Japan** in Osaka is the same. Either one is a hard squeeze on this trip, and easy to slot in once you have the extra days.
- **Hokkaido**, if you can spare 3 or 4 days. The northern island is a different Japan: **Sapporo** and its food, **Otaru**'s canal town, **Furano** and **Biei** for lavender in summer, and some of the world's best powder snow in winter. It is a flight from the main island, so it is its own leg, but worth building a longer trip around.
- Other add-ons worth a look with two-plus weeks: **Kanazawa** (gardens and old districts), **Mt. Koya** (temple-stay among the cedars), and **Takayama** in the Japanese Alps.
